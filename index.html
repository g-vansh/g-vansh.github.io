Where do ideas come from?

I'm Vansh Gupta — innovation economist and PhD student at MIT Sloan. I survey the territory where new ideas appear: the incentives, places, genes, and governments behind them.

01The surveyor field notes, first person

Economics treats ideas as the engine of growth, then files their origins under “exogenous.”

I think that's a cop-out, so I study the origins directly. My current work follows the question across four terrains: what happens when you pay people for ideas (a $20 GitHub sponsorship can quietly crowd out the community work that open source runs on); how local governments in Brazil build — and lose — the capacity that lets ideas survive floods; how proximity moves invention between people; and how deep the roots go, down to genes. Underneath all four runs the same problem: ideas are public goods, and we still don't really know how to provide them.

I got here the long way: two degrees at Cornell (biometry & statistics, applied economics), a reproducibility stint at the American Economic Association, antitrust data science on hospital mergers at Charles River Associates, and now the TIES PhD at MIT Sloan. Along the route I kept building things — R packages, mapping tools, LLM pipelines that read sixteen million paragraphs of Brazilian municipal gazettes — because the questions I like are the ones existing instruments can't measure yet.

Off the clock I collect cities and their transit systems, draw maps nobody asked for, and am perpetually one attempt away from biking hands-free.

Place

Ideas cluster on the map. How much of invention is the person, and how much is standing in the right place, near the right people?

Government

Ideas need ground to stand on. With 16M paragraphs of municipal gazettes, we measure what Brazilian local governments actually do — and what floods do to them.
03Selected work three readings from the field

Better Keep the Twenty Dollars: Incentivizing Innovation in Open Source

w/ Annamaria Conti, Jorge Guzman & Maria Roche · R&R at Management Science · NBER WP 31668

100,000 GitHub developers, one natural experiment. Receiving sponsorship money has a long-lasting negative effect on innovation — extrinsic rewards crowd out the community work open source depends on.

NBER NBER Bulletin HBS Working Knowledge

Local Government State Capacity: Evidence from Brazil

w/ Michael Best, Renata Lemos & Daniela Scur · in progress

Every Brazilian municipality publishes a daily gazette. We read all of them — 16.4 million paragraphs, labelled with an LLM cascade — to build the first daily measure of local government at work.

Municipal Responses to Natural Disasters: Evidence from Brazil

w/ Michael Best, Renata Lemos & Daniela Scur · in progress

When the floods come, what does a local government stop doing — and for how long? We follow what gets dropped and what comes back, day by day.
